### A. Gordon optical metric

**Support.**  
Gordon's optical metric gives the historical foundation for representing light propagation in media through effective metric geometry.

**Use.**  
Supports the essay's claim that optical media provide genuine effective-geometric null-ray models.

**Boundary.**

### B. Transformation optics and geometry of light

**Support.**  
Transformation optics uses designed electromagnetic media to implement geometrical control of light propagation and explicitly connects optical media with geometrical ideas from general relativity.

**Use.**

**Boundary.**  
This supports optical geometry, not direct spacetime realization.

### C. Fabricated graded-index optics

**Support.**  
Graded-index optical systems with finite refractive-index ranges are physically realizable in glass and polymer systems.

**Use.**  
Supports the essay's bounded optical-profile example as physically motivated rather than purely formal.

**Boundary.**  
This does not verify the exact profile \(n(r)=1-\varepsilon e^{-r/R}\) as a manufactured device.

Primary source:

- N. Vaidya et al., “Immersion graded index optics: theory, design, and prototypes,” Microsystems & Nanoengineering 8, 60 (2022).  
  https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9234043/

### D. Energy conditions as admissibility filters

**Support.**  
Energy conditions are physically motivated restrictions on stress-energy, but pointwise conditions face systematic limitations in quantum field theory. Averaged and quantum conditions are used as weaker filters.

**Use.**  
Supports the essay's distinction between material energy-condition filters and operational finite-capacity filters.

**Boundary.**  
This does not establish finite capacity as a standard replacement for energy conditions.

Primary source:

- E.-A. Kontou and K. Sanders, “Energy conditions in general relativity and quantum field theory,” Classical and Quantum Gravity 37, 193001 (2020).  
  https://arxiv.org/abs/2003.01815

---

### E. Quantum inequalities and magnitude-duration limits

**Support.**  
Quantum inequalities constrain negative energy by magnitude-duration tradeoffs.

**Use.**  
Supports the essay's core exclusion principle: large or persistent exotic-looking effects require compensating bounds rather than unbounded operational gain.

**Boundary.**  
Quantum inequalities do not directly imply the finite-capacity inequality used in the essay.

Primary sources:

- L. H. Ford and T. A. Roman, “Averaged Energy Conditions and Quantum Inequalities,” Physical Review D 51, 4277–4286 (1995).  
  https://arxiv.org/abs/gr-qc/9410043

- C. J. Fewster, “Lectures on quantum energy inequalities,” arXiv:1208.5399.  
  https://arxiv.org/pdf/1208.5399

### F. Operational interpretation of negative energy

**Support.**  
Helfer argues that operationally detecting or capturing negative local energy requires compensating positive detector energy.

**Use.**  
Supports interpreting finite capacity as an operational admissibility principle.

**Boundary.**  
This is conceptual support, not a proof of the essay's finite-capacity axiom.

Primary source:

- A. D. Helfer, “On Quantum Inequalities,” arXiv:gr-qc/9612029.  
  https://arxiv.org/abs/gr-qc/9612029
